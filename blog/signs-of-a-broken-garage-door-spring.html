7 Signs Your Garage Door Spring Is Broken

Your garage door spring is the hardest-working component in the entire system. It counterbalances the weight of the door, hundreds of times a year, so the opener motor only has to guide the door rather than haul it. When a spring fails, the whole system stops working. In San Diego County, where salt air from communities like Ocean Beach, La Jolla, and Pacific Beach accelerates metal corrosion, springs often wear out faster than the national average. Knowing how to spot the warning signs early can prevent a stuck door, a burned-out opener, or a dangerous drop.

How Garage Door Springs Work

Most San Diego homes use one of two spring configurations. Torsion springs mount horizontally above the door opening on a steel shaft. As the door closes, the spring winds tighter, storing energy that releases when the door needs to open again. Extension springs run along the horizontal tracks on each side of the door and stretch as the door descends.

Both types operate under enormous mechanical tension. A torsion spring on a typical two-car garage door may hold several hundred pounds of stored force. That tension is exactly why a spring failure often sounds like a gunshot: the stored energy releases all at once when the coil snaps.

The 7 Warning Signs

1. The Door Won't Open at All

This is the most common symptom. You press the remote or wall button, the opener motor runs, and nothing happens. Without the spring doing the heavy lifting, the motor simply cannot move the door. You may hear the motor hum, strain, then stop. If the opener activates but the door stays put, a broken spring is the first thing to check.

2. A Loud Bang From the Garage

Homeowners often describe a snapping torsion spring as sounding like a firecracker or a rifle shot inside the garage. The sound comes from the spring uncoiling violently when it breaks. If you heard a sudden loud bang and the door no longer works, check the horizontal bar above the door opening for a visible gap in the spring coil. A break is usually obvious once you know what to look for.

3. A Visible Gap in the Spring

Stand inside the garage with the door closed and look at the torsion spring mounted above the door. A healthy spring is a continuous, tightly-wound coil. A broken spring will have a gap of one to three inches where the metal separated. Do not touch the spring or try to operate the door. This is the most definitive visual confirmation of a break.

4. The Door Opens Crooked or Only a Few Inches

Extension spring systems use one spring on each side of the door. When only one breaks, the door gets uneven lift: one side goes up while the other barely moves. The result is a door that tilts to one side, opens only partway, or gets stuck in the tracks. Left unaddressed, this imbalance can cause the cables to slip off the drums and leave you with a garage door that comes off track, which is a separate repair.

5. The Door Falls Fast When Closing

A properly functioning door lowers smoothly, easing to a gentle stop near the floor. Spring tension provides controlled resistance throughout the descent. If your door drops or slams closed instead of easing down, the springs are no longer doing their job. This is a safety hazard, particularly in garages used by children or pets. A slamming door can also crack the bottom seal, bend the door frame, and damage the concrete below.

6. Slack or Dangling Cables

The lifting cables connect the torsion shaft to the bottom corners of the door. When a spring breaks, the tension that keeps those cables taut disappears. One or both cables may go slack, pile up at the base of the door, or hang loosely along the side tracks. Loose cables are a secondary sign of a spring problem and can tangle in the drums if you try to force the door open. If you see hanging garage door cables, stop using the door immediately.

7. The Opener Trips Its Safety Overload

Modern openers include a built-in force sensor that stops the motor if the door is too heavy to move safely. When a spring is broken or near failure, the door becomes too heavy and the opener's overload protection kicks in. The door may reverse back down after moving just a few inches, or the opener light may flash a specific error code. If your opener is consistently reversing or stopping mid-travel, do not assume it is the opener itself: check the springs first.

What to Do (and What NOT to Do) When a Spring Breaks

The most important rule: do not try to open the door manually or force it with the opener. A door without spring tension can weigh 150 to 400 pounds. Without counterbalance, lifting it by hand is dangerous, and running the opener against a stuck door risks burning out the motor or snapping a cable.

Here is what to do instead:

  • Leave the door in the closed position if possible. This is the safest configuration when a spring is broken.
  • Disengage the opener by pulling the red emergency release cord, and do NOT attempt to raise the door by hand.
  • If your car is trapped inside, call Noah Garage Doors at (619) 572-4266 for same-day service. We can usually reach most San Diego neighborhoods within a few hours, and we handle emergency garage door repair in San Diego around the clock.
  • Do not attempt a DIY spring replacement. Torsion springs are wound under hundreds of pounds of force. Without the correct tools and training, the spring can release violently during installation, causing serious injury.

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens to my garage door when a spring breaks?

When a spring breaks, the door loses its counterbalance and becomes extremely heavy. The opener motor stalls because it cannot lift the door on its own. Most homeowners hear a loud bang at the moment of failure and then find the door stuck closed or sitting unevenly in the tracks.

Is it safe to use a garage door with a broken spring?

No, it is not safe. Operating a door with a broken spring can burn out the opener motor, cause the cables to snap, or allow the door to fall suddenly. Do not try to open the door manually either, as a door without spring tension can weigh 150 to 400 pounds and is very difficult to control safely. How long do garage door springs last in San Diego?

Most standard springs are rated for 10,000 cycles, which equals roughly 7 to 10 years of daily use.
