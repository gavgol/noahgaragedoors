The Most Common Garage Door Emergencies We See in San Diego

Garage door emergencies come in several forms. Understanding which situation you're dealing with helps you respond correctly until a technician arrives.

Door Stuck Closed — Car Trapped Inside

This is the scenario that causes the most immediate disruption. The door won't open, your vehicle is inside, and you're going to be late. This is typically caused by a broken torsion spring, a snapped cable, a dead opener, or a track that has shifted out of alignment. Do not repeatedly pound the wall button or hold the remote button hoping the door forces itself open. This can burn out the opener motor or cause a cable to snap completely.

Door Stuck Open — Home Left Exposed

A door frozen in the open position is a security and safety emergency. Your home, vehicles, and belongings are exposed to theft, and if it happens overnight, you're dealing with a genuine vulnerability. This situation often involves a broken spring (the door has no counterbalance to hold it down), a trolley that has jumped off its track, or a logic board failure in the opener.

Spring Broke Overnight

Many San Diego homeowners wake up to a loud bang that happened sometime during the night — that's the sound of a torsion spring breaking under tension. The door may appear normal until you try to open it, at which point the opener strains and the door lifts only a few inches before stopping. This is a very common call across neighborhoods from Mission Valley to La Mesa and Lemon Grove.

Door Off the Tracks

An impact from a vehicle, a worn roller that finally gave out, or a cable that unwound can send the door off its tracks. This situation looks alarming but is repairable. The danger is attempting to operate the door when it's off-track — doing so can cause the panels to buckle or the door to drop.

Important Safety Warning:

In all emergency situations, disconnect the automatic opener (pull the red emergency release cord hanging from the trolley) and do not attempt to manually force the door open or closed. A garage door weighs 150 to 400 pounds. Without proper spring tension, it can fall without warning.

What NOT To Do in a Garage Door Emergency

We've seen the aftermath of homeowners trying to solve these problems themselves, and it's almost always more expensive and sometimes dangerous. Here's what to avoid:

  • Don't force the door open or closed — If the spring is broken, the full weight of the door is unsupported. Forcing it risks injury and can damage panels, cables, and the opener carriage.
  • Don't try to wind or unwind a spring yourself — Torsion springs are under enormous tension. This is a job for trained technicians with the right tools, period.
  • Don't repeatedly activate the opener — If the door isn't moving freely, running the motor repeatedly will burn it out and turn a $300 repair into a $600 repair.
  • Don't ignore a door that's only partially open — A door balanced on a broken spring or loose cable can come down without warning.

How to Safely Use the Manual Release

If your car is trapped and you need to get out, you can safely disengage the automatic opener using the emergency release. Here's how to do it correctly:

  1. Make sure the door is fully closed before pulling the release. If the door is partially open with a broken spring, do not attempt this — call us instead.
  2. Locate the red emergency release cord hanging from the trolley (the moving part on the overhead track). It usually has a red handle.
  3. Pull the cord straight down (and slightly toward the door) until you hear a click. This disconnects the door from the drive carriage.
  4. Lift the door manually, using both hands, lifting evenly from the bottom center. If it feels extremely heavy or won't move, stop — the spring may be broken and the door is not safe to lift without assistance.
  5. Prop the door open if you need to drive out, using a locking clamp on the track or having someone hold it. Never leave a door propped open unattended if the spring is broken.
